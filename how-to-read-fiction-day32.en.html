DAY 32

Art & Aesthetics: How to Read Fiction & Prose

July 17, 2026 · Perception Training, Lesson 32

Last time we learned how to read a poem — slow down, hear the sound, stay inside the image. Today we widen the lens to longer prose: fiction and the essay. Many people read a novel only to chase the plot — "and then? and then?" — racing to the end and closing the book remembering nothing but a summary. Yet what is truly good in a novel often lies not in what is told but in how: who tells it, in what order, in what kind of sentences. The essay is even more so — it may have almost no plot, standing entirely on the texture of its language. Four things today: locate the narrator, see how time is arranged, taste the voice of the prose, and finally use "close reading" to draw the richness out of a single passage.

POINT 01

Point of View & Distance

Who Is Telling, and How Close
[How to Read]
  1. First ask "who is telling this story." First person ("I"), limited third (riding along with one character), or omniscient (able to enter every mind)? Locate where the narrator stands before anything else — only then do you know whose version you're hearing, and whose version is being kept from you.
  2. Then ask "how close." Is the camera pressed inside the character's head, hearing their private thoughts, or standing far off, filming only gesture and expression? Up close, you find yourself identifying with them; far away, you watch them coolly. Distance decides whether you take sides or observe.
  3. Notice whether the narrator can be trusted. A first-person "I" doesn't always tell the truth — they embellish, conceal, deceive themselves. Don't take every line of the narrator as the author's judgment; between the two there is often a gap, and that gap is where irony lives.
[Example]

Hemingway's "Hills Like White Elephants": almost nothing but dialogue and action; the author never tells you what the characters are thinking. What to watch: the point of view deliberately clings to the "surface," draining away all interior life, forcing you — like someone eavesdropping on a couple — to guess from what they say and what they carefully avoid saying. This is the "dramatic" point of view. From China: Lu Xun's "Kong Yiji" is told by a young tavern boy, "I." A half-comprehending child's eyes — precisely because he doesn't fully grasp Kong Yiji's sorrow, that sorrow cuts all the deeper.

[Common Misconception]

Taking "I" as the author in person, and every line of the narrator as the novel's final verdict. In fact the narrator is only a "camera position" the author has sent out — where that camera sits, and whether it's reliable, is itself the author speaking.

[Try It Yourself]

Take a short story you know well and do just one thing: decide its person and how close the camera sits to the protagonist. Then imagine — if someone else told it (say, "Kong Yiji" narrated by Kong Yiji himself), what would the story become? You'll feel the weight of point of view at once.

In one line: Point of view isn't a technical detail — it's the position you've been placed in; what you see and whom you trust are quietly decided by it.

To ponder: Why does the same event, told by different people, become a different story?

POINT 02

Structure & Rhythm

How Time Gets Arranged
[How to Read]
  1. Separate "story time" from "telling time." The story itself happens in natural order (1-2-3), but the author may scramble it (giving you 3 first, then circling back to 1). Watch for flashback, digression, and jump-cut — whichever moment the author moves to the front is the one they want you to remember first.
  2. Reading "pace and proportion" is reading rhythm. The author spends five pages on a single meal, dispatches ten years in one sentence — what's stretched out is the center of gravity; what's compressed or skipped (ellipsis) often hides what most deserves your thought. Where it slows and where it fast-forwards is the author's drumbeat.
  3. Find "the shape of the structure." A good work often has a hidden skeleton: an echoing opening and close, mirrored scenes, a recurring image. When you finish, look back and ask "why does it begin here and end there" — and the shape surfaces.
[Example]

The famous first sentence of García Márquez's "One Hundred Years of Solitude" packs the future (facing the firing squad), the past (that distant afternoon), and the present into a single line — time folded together. What to watch: feel how, in one sentence alone, the author hurls you into a circular, fated sense of time. From China: Lu Xun's "The New Year's Sacrifice" uses reverse order — it opens with Xianglin's Wife already dead, then circles back to how, step by step, she was driven there. Because the ending is set before you first, reading becomes watching her walk toward a death you already know — that heaviness is made by structure.

[Common Misconception]

Thinking "plot" equals "structure." Plot is what happens; structure is how those events are arranged, in what order they're shown to you. The same heap of events, told forward, is a chronicle; told backward, it may become a tragedy.

[Try It Yourself]

Pick a short story you've read and draw its "double timeline": the story's real order on top, the author's telling order below, connecting the lines to see where they diverge. Which passage was moved forward, which crushed into a single sentence? Those dislocations are the author's design.

In one line: Structure isn't about telling things clearly — it's about deciding what you see first and what is saved for last. The order itself is meaning.

To ponder: Why does the same heap of events, told forward versus backward, become two different stories?

POINT 03

The Texture of Language

Prose Has a Voice
[How to Read]
  1. Read a passage aloud. Prose has sound too: are the sentences short and clipped, or long and unhurried? Only aloud do you hear the rhythm. Hemingway's short sentences are like nails driven into wood; Faulkner's long ones like a rising tide — for the same event, the length of the sentence is itself the emotion.
  2. Watch "concrete versus abstract." Good prose usually stands on concrete nouns and verbs, sparing with adjectives. Notice whether the author sets the image directly before you, or busies themselves with words like "beautiful" and "moving," passing the verdict down for you in advance.
  3. Taste "restraint and the unsaid." The best writing often doesn't say everything. Wang Zengqi writes an event plainly, as if nothing were said at all — yet that ease and cleanness is exactly the flavor. Don't only hunt for gorgeous sentences; learning to admire the "plain" is a high order of taste.
[Example]

Shen Congwen's "Border Town" is written as clear as water, describing the landscape and people of western Hunan without a ripple — the calmer it is, the deeper the feeling. What to watch: notice how he barely uses big words or sentiment, only laying out scene and person stroke by stroke, the emotion seeping out from between the words. From the classics: Zhang Dai's "Viewing Snow from the Heart of the Lake Pavilion" — "rime hung thick, sky and cloud and mountain and water, all one white above and below" — in a handful of characters a lonely, cold snow-night stands complete. Western contrast: Hemingway's telegraphic style and Proust's sentences unrolling across a full page are two utterly different "voices," each with its own breathing.

[Common Misconception]

Thinking good writing means "lavish diction, one quotable line after another." Quite the opposite — piling up adjectives is often a lack of confidence. Truly good writing is often exact, spare, every word sitting where it should.

[Try It Yourself]

Find a passage you think is "really well written" and copy it out by hand — slowly. As you copy you'll notice what you usually read past: the choice of a single verb, a pause, a place that refuses to say everything. Copying is the slowest and deepest close reading.

In one line: Half of prose's content is in "what is said," the other half in "how" — the length and speed of the sentence is its voice and its body heat.

To ponder: Is "good writing" ornate or clean? Why is piling up diction so often a giveaway of weakness?

POINT 04

Close Reading & East–West

Reading One Passage to the Bottom — and Two Traditions Side by Side
[How to Read]
  1. Pick a small passage and read it to death. Close reading seeks not speed but slowness: for one paragraph, ask word by word "why this word and not a near-synonym," "why break the sentence here," "why did the author give this detail." Magnified to the extreme, a single passage yields ninety percent of what fast reading missed.
  2. Fix your eye on "the detail." An object, a gesture, the weather that seems tossed off is often no idle stroke. Watch what recurs, and ask what it's carrying — when Cao Xueqin writes a jade pendant or a crab feast, he is never merely writing about food.
  3. Learn to "read across." Set two traditions side by side and each one's character shows. Classical Chinese fiction favors "plain-line drawing" and outward action (Dream of the Red Chamber rarely states psychology directly, hinting it all through dialogue, action, and verse); Western modern fiction invented "stream of consciousness," diving straight into the mind and writing the thoughts down like flowing water (Woolf, Joyce). Both roads reach the human heart, by wholly different routes.
[Example]

When Dream of the Red Chamber describes Daiyu entering the Jia household, it is all through her eyes upon this strange great family: the rules, the furnishings, the expressions on faces — all "seen," the psychology hidden in her watchfulness and care, almost never stated. What to watch: how Cao Xueqin uses outward detail (how she bows, how she navigates the etiquette) to let you read out the sensitivity of one living under another's roof. Western contrast: in Woolf's "Mrs Dalloway," across a single day the characters' thoughts flow, leap, and associate without stop, the author spreading the "thinking" open before you. One hides the heart inside action, the other pours it straight out — two roads for "how to write a person."

[Common Misconception]

Thinking close reading is "overreading" — "the author never thought that hard." But close reading trains not the guessing of the author's mind; it trains your own capacity to see finer and slower. Even where a touch was unintentional, how much a good detail can be read out of is the reader's craft.

[Try It Yourself]

This week pick your favorite paragraph in a short story (half a page is enough) and read it five times: once straight through, once aloud, once circling every concrete noun, once asking whether each verb could be swapped, once with the book closed, recalling the image it left. You'll find a paragraph is far richer than you thought.

In one line: Close reading is placing a small piece of text under the greatest magnification, slow enough to hear each word at work — the foundation of all "knowing how to read."

To ponder: Classical Chinese fiction rarely states psychology directly — why do its characters feel so vividly alive?

Deeper Thought

Why is "who is telling the story" sometimes more important than "what story is told"?
Because the same event, told by a different person, becomes a different story. The event is dead; the narrator is alive — where they stand, their bias, how much they're willing to say all quietly fit you with tinted glasses. Told by Kong Yiji himself, "Kong Yiji" becomes self-pity; told by a half-comprehending tavern boy in passing, the pathos of a man treated as everyone's joke finally shows. So in reading fiction, don't rush to ask "what happened" — first ask "whose version is this, and why do they tell it this way."
Why does the same heap of events, told forward versus backward, become two stories?
Because order decides the mood in which you experience each step. Told forward, you, like the characters, are ignorant of the future — you read "suspense." Told backward, the ending handed to you first (as when "The New Year's Sacrifice" opens with Xianglin's Wife already dead), reading becomes watching her walk toward a known abyss; suspense gone, fated heaviness in its place. The same facts: one makes you anticipate, one makes you mourn. A novel's power often lies not in the "events" but in the order in which they're laid out.
Is "good writing" ornate or clean? Why is piling up diction so often a giveaway of weakness?
Mature good writing is mostly clean, not ornate. Ornament is easy — memorize adjectives, string together parallels. Hard is the exact and the spare: the most fitting verb, saying what should be said and not one word more. Zhang Dai renders a snow-night in "all one white above and below"; Wang Zengqi writes plain as water, the power in the restraint. The thicker the adjectives, often the more the author themselves failed to see clearly, reaching for big words like "so very beautiful" to pass the verdict down for the reader. To learn to admire the "plain" is to clear a threshold of taste.
Classical Chinese fiction rarely states a character's psychology, yet the characters feel utterly alive — how?
Through "plain-line drawing" — not entering the mind, only setting out the action, speech, expression, and surrounding detail stroke by stroke, letting you infer the interior from outside. Daiyu's "care at every step, watchfulness at every moment" is written entirely in how she bows and navigates etiquette, not one line of interior monologue, yet a sensitive, proud dependent leaps off the page. This is the opposite of Western "stream of consciousness": one hides the heart inside action and makes you infer; one pours it straight out for you to see. Two answers, no hierarchy, to "how to write a person."
In an age used to "finishing a book in three minutes," what does reading one paragraph word by word actually train?
It trains a capacity now growing scarce: to hold attention steadily on one small thing, long enough that it begins to speak back. Speed-reading, book-summaries, three-minute recaps give you the gist and the conclusion, yet skip exactly what is truly good in a novel — the things hidden in the breathing of sentences, the choice of words, the weight of details, which only slowness can catch. Close reading trains not only reading but, in an age that chops, accelerates, and spoon-feeds everything, reclaiming the initiative to look slowly and think slowly for yourself.
