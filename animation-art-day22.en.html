DAY 22

Art & Aesthetics: The Art of Animation

July 5, 2026 · Perception Training, Lesson 22

The most magical thing about animation is that it conjures "life" out of nothing. A lump of clay, a stroke of ink, a hand-drawn line — dead things — can make you believe they breathe, are afraid. This doesn't come from drawing like a photograph, but from a whole craft of "how motion becomes believable." Today's four keys — Disney's Arc, the World of Miyazaki, Stop-Motion, Animation as Expression — train an eye that sees the real feeling inside a fake movement: understanding animation begins when you stop asking "does it look real?" and start asking "how did it make me believe?"

POINT 01

Disney's Arc

The Illusion of Life
【How to Look】
  1. Trace the path of a motion with your eyes: in reality, almost nothing moves in a straight line. A swinging arm, a turning head, a bouncing ball all travel along arcs. Follow a movement with your gaze and you'll find it's a graceful curve — that's Disney's first rule.
  2. Watch the "anticipation" before a big action: a fist pulls back before it punches, knees bend before a jump, a body sways left before dashing right. A character almost never fires from nowhere — a small counter-move always "previews" the action. That's where the sense of rhythm comes from.
  3. Watch "squash & stretch": a ball flattens on impact and lengthens as it rises — the core magic that lends weight and elasticity. The bouncier a thing, the more it deforms. Stare at the impact frame: does it get "squashed"?
  4. Watch "follow-through": the body stops, but the coat-tail, the tail, the hair keep swinging. A real body isn't a rigid plank; its parts lag one behind another.
  5. Watch the timing — "slow in & out": an action usually starts slow, speeds up in the middle, and eases out. An even speed looks mechanical.
【Works to Watch】

Disney's Bambi (1942): the "Nine Old Men" animation team honed these rules to perfection, and two of them later distilled them into the famous "Twelve Principles of Animation." Watch Bambi slip and struggle to stand on the ice — every stumble uses anticipation, arcs, and follow-through with precision. (Copyrighted; view through official Disney channels.)

Steamboat Willie (1928): the first sound cartoon starring Mickey Mouse, now in the public domain and free to watch online. Watch him bob his head to the beat, his whole body "bouncing" — the marriage of animation and sound starts here.

【Common Misconception】

Thinking that good animation means drawing as close to real people and objects as possible. Quite the opposite: Disney never chased "realism" but "believable exaggeration" — a ball squashed flatter than reality, an expression bigger than a real face, and you believe it more. Realism is the camera's job; animation lives or dies on the believability of motion, not the fidelity of the image.

【Try It Yourself】

Shoot a slow-motion clip of a ping-pong ball bouncing on your phone and go frame by frame: at the moment of impact, does the ball get "squashed"? You'll suddenly grasp that the animator is simply enlarging, for you, these fleeting moments that reality flashes past.

In a sentence: Animation isn't about drawing the world accurately, but about making motion believable — arcs, anticipation, squash, all to make the fake move like the real.
To ponder: If animation's charm lies in "believable exaggeration," has CG's relentless chase of photographic realism thrown away its home-field skill?
POINT 02

The World of Hayao Miyazaki

The World of Hayao Miyazaki
【How to Look】
  1. Deliberately hunt for shots where "nothing happens": a character gazing at the sky, wind crossing a field of grass, ripples spreading over water. Japanese aesthetics calls this ma (間) — the pause of emptiness. Don't fast-forward; that's the space the director leaves for emotion to breathe. Count: after a big climax, how many seconds of quiet does he give you?
  2. Watch the flight. Nearly every film has a flying sequence, drawn not as superhero weightlessness but as flight with mass, wind resistance, effort — that sense of weight is the signature of his craft.
  3. Watch the everyday chores: frying an egg, cooking soup, sweeping the floor. It's precisely these "useless" daily details that make a fantasy world tangible and believable.
  4. Watch the ambiguity of the villains: his worlds have almost no pure evil. The "enemy" usually carries a side you can understand, even sympathize with.
【Works to Watch】

My Neighbor Totoro (1988): watch the scene where the sisters wait for their father at the rainy bus stop and Totoro quietly appears. Almost no dialogue — just rain, silence, and one umbrella. A textbook of ma.

Spirited Away (2001, Academy Award for Best Animated Feature): watch the few minutes when Chihiro rides the train over the water. Outside is water and dusk; not a word is spoken inside — yet it's one of the film's most moving passages.

These films are made by Studio Ghibli and truly exist; as copyrighted works, view them through legitimate channels.
【Common Misconception】

Thinking Miyazaki = cute, healing, fairy tales for kids. His core is often heavy: the scars of war, the ruin of the environment, the price of growing up. Spirited Away is about how a child holds onto her own name in a greedy adult world — under the adorable skin is a weighty theme meant for grown-ups.

【Try It Yourself】

Rewatch that train-over-the-sea passage in Spirited Away (about three minutes). Forget the plot; watch only three things: how the color of the light shifts, how the water reflects, how long the silence lasts. Feel how animation, with no dialogue at all — only "stillness" — can slip an inexpressible homesickness into your heart.

In a sentence: Half of Miyazaki's magic is the spectacle of flight, the other half is those "nothing-happens" empty shots — he knows to leave a breathing space for emotion.
To ponder: Miyazaki insists on hand-drawing, Pixar goes all-CG — where does their difference in "warmth" lie? Does more advanced technology always make animation more moving?
POINT 03

Stop-Motion

Stop-Motion
【How to Look】
  1. First remind yourself: every frame on screen is a real physical object — clay, a puppet, cut paper — nudged a fraction by a hand, then photographed. One second of footage is 12 to 24 rounds of "nudge, shoot" — perhaps two or three seconds is all an animator produces in a whole day.
  2. Watch the "truth" of materials: fingerprints in clay, the fuzz of felt, the grain of wood — real reflections under real light. This is the great divide from CG: CG's smoothness is computed, stop-motion's texture is touchable.
  3. Watch that faint "jitter": in stop-motion, movements carry a tiny unsteadiness. It's not a flaw but the breathing of countless hands placing frame after frame — the body heat that sets it apart from a machine.
  4. Watch the weight: real objects have real mass; drops and collisions obey real physics — a solidity that drawn animation struggles to fake.
【Works to Watch】

The Nightmare Before Christmas (1993, produced by Tim Burton, directed by Henry Selick): a puppet stop-motion classic — watch how Jack's lanky body is given exaggerated yet elegant movement. Aardman's Wallace & Gromit series: the standard-bearer of clay stop-motion — notice the clay mark at the corner of a mouth, thumbed into place and always subtly shifting.

Shanghai Animation Film Studio's The Magic Brush (Ma Liang) (1955, directed by Jin Xi): the founding work of Chinese puppet animation. In the way Ma Liang holds his brush and walks, feel that slightly rustic sense of weight — a precious heritage of Eastern stop-motion.

All the above truly exist. The first two are copyrighted works, viewable through legitimate channels; The Magic Brush is an early Shanghai puppet film with official restorations available.
【Common Misconception】

Thinking stop-motion is crude, dated, a poor substitute for those who can't afford CG. Exactly the reverse: many directors, in an age when CG is at their fingertips, still choose stop-motion — for that "material reality" CG cannot copy. You know that lump of clay was really placed by a hand, and this "clumsiness" is itself moving.

【Try It Yourself】

Search for a stop-motion behind-the-scenes clip and watch the animator bend over a miniature set, nudging a puppet's finger, blinking an eye, frame by frame. Go back to the film after, and you'll feel a fresh reverence for every second that flows past.

In a sentence: Every second of stop-motion is a real object moved by a hand dozens of times — its imperfection is precisely its body heat.
To ponder: In an age when CG can produce any image, why does the "handmade jitter" that stop-motion keeps actually move us more?
POINT 04

Animation as Expression

Animation as Expression
【How to Look】
  1. First correct a deep-rooted notion: animation is a medium, not a genre — just as oil painting doesn't equal one kind of subject. It can carry comedy, and it can carry death, war, homesickness. As you watch, throw away the assumption "it's animation, so it's for children."
  2. Deliberately look for what live-action can't do: metamorphosis, the direct visualization of metaphor. When sorrow turns into falling snow, longing into a river — that's animation's own channel of expression, one that bypasses language.
  3. Watch "auteur animation" (personal independent works): they don't chase Hollywood-style slick virtuosity but let style itself become content — how the image is drawn is what the director means.
  4. Watch the aesthetic roots of different cultures: Japan's mono no aware, Europe's experiments, China's ink — one medium, blossoming into utterly different flowers.
【Works to Watch】

The ink-wash animation Where Is Mama (Little Tadpoles Look for Their Mother) (1960, Shanghai Animation): the world's first ink-wash animation, drawing on the spirit of Qi Baishi, making ink dots on rice paper truly swim. Watch how the density of the ink and the bleeding edges keep the "breath" of ink painting even while moving — a first no other country had attempted. Havoc in Heaven (1961–64, directed by Wan Laiming) made Peking-opera masks and decorative patterns the emblem of the "Chinese Animation School."

Yuri Norstein's Hedgehog in the Fog (1975, USSR): often voted by peers one of the greatest animated shorts in history. A little hedgehog walks into thick fog, the image hazy and poetic — it tells not a story but fear, loneliness, and curiosity about the world itself.

All the above truly exist. Where Is Mama and Havoc in Heaven have official Shanghai restorations available; Hedgehog in the Fog has legitimate releases. Adult-oriented works like Satoshi Kon's Millennium Actress are copyrighted; view through legitimate channels.
【Common Misconception】

Thinking animation is film's "juvenile branch," something to outgrow for live-action. In many parts of the world, animation has long been one of the most serious forms of art: it can draw the inner life and dreams a camera can never reach. To treat it as mere children's entertainment is to shut a door on ourselves.

【Try It Yourself】

Watch Where Is Mama or Hedgehog in the Fog all the way through, noticing that neither chases "looking real," yet both grip you more than many realistic images. Ask yourself afterward — what moved me just now, the story, or that indescribable atmosphere only animation can give?

In a sentence: Animation is a medium, not a genre — it can draw the inner life a camera can't reach, a channel that bypasses language and lands straight on feeling.
To ponder: Why does the same craft grow into serious mainstream expression in East Asia, yet stay locked in the "children's entertainment" drawer in much of the West?

Deeper Reflection

Is animation's pursuit of photorealism progress, or a wrong turn?
The heart of Disney's rules is "believable exaggeration": enlarging the fleeting moments of reality so the fake moves more convincingly than the real. Believable isn't the same as photo-accurate — a ball squashed flatter than reality makes you believe more in its bounce. When CG renders water and hair indistinguishable from a photo, it's worth asking: is it strengthening that "believability," or replacing it with mere accuracy? Perhaps between realism and being moved lies a layer of "just-right fakery."
Where exactly does the "warmth" of hand-drawing differ from CG's "perfection"?
Every hand-drawn frame comes from a human hand; lines quiver slightly, proportions wander a little, and these "imprecisions" accumulate into a breathing sense of life. CG is computed precisely — smooth, stable, reproducible. The difference may lie not in beauty but in "trace": hand-drawing keeps the human trace, CG erases it. Miyazaki insists on hand-drawing, Pixar embraces CG, and both roads yield masterpieces — proof that the source of moving us can differ. The real question may be: when technology can smooth away every trace, are we still willing to keep a little evidence that "a person did this"?
Why does stop-motion's "imperfection" move us more?
That faint jitter in stop-motion is the physical trace of countless hands placing frame after frame. It moves us perhaps because we subconsciously know "this truly took effort" — behind every second is imaginable patience and labor. Flawless smoothness earns admiration but not easy empathy; roughness with the mark of a hand feels intimate. It's the same psychology by which we love a handmade bowl over an assembly-line one: within the imperfection hides a specific person.
Does a form as hard to mass-produce as ink-wash animation still matter in an age chasing efficiency?
The labor Where Is Mama consumed looks almost "not worth it" today — every bleed of ink had to be hand-layered. But precisely because it's hard, it became an irreplaceable cultural specimen: proof that animation need not wear only Hollywood's face, that a nation's painting tradition can live directly inside a new medium. Efficiency answers "faster, cheaper, more," but not "resembling whom, unique why." Perhaps it's exactly these "unprofitable" forms that keep a culture's own face.
Training perception, not stacking facts.
